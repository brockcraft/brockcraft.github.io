The headline: You have a room of motivated students who skew UX Design, most of whom are continuing in HCD rather than career-switching. The majority have some prior tool exposure but limited writing ability in code. The single biggest anxiety is "not being technical enough," and almost three-quarters already use AI coding assistants — making this cohort unusually well-positioned for a vibecoding-forward curriculum.

Build Deployable Work & Portfolio

The dominant theme: students want to finish the quarter with something functional they can show. Portfolio artifacts, deployed prototypes, and "something people can actually use" appear across nearly all responses.

~18 students

Code Literacy & Confidence

Students want to cross from "intimidated by code" to "able to read, debug, and modify it." Many frame success as no longer feeling blocked when they encounter code in collaborative work.

~14 students

AI-Augmented Workflows

Explicit interest in learning to work with AI tools deliberately — prompting well, judging AI output quality, and building fluency with the Claude/Cursor stack. Several mention wanting to understand what the AI actually generated.

~12 students

Connect Code to HCD Practice

Students want technical skills that translate directly: talking to engineers, supporting data-informed design decisions, analyzing research data programmatically, or automating repetitive tasks in their existing workflows.

~8 students

Data Analysis & Visualization

A subset — particularly PM and UX Research students — are focused on the data side: pulling, cleaning, and visualizing data for stakeholders. Several mention this as a career-differentiating skill.

~6 students

Self-Sufficiency with Technical Stakeholders

Career-changers and PM students especially want the fluency to hold their own in technical conversations with engineers — understanding requirements, reviewing code, and not needing to outsource basic debugging.

What They're Nervous About

Concerns (multiple selections, n = 35)

"Not technical enough" was selected by 23 of 35 students (66%) — by far the most common concern. "Learning not sticking" (40%) and "working in public on GitHub" (43%) are the next tier. Only 20% said they're not nervous at all. The pace concern (40%) is worth watching: this cohort has prior experience with code not sticking, so early wins matter.

Teaching Notes

  • Address the "not technical enough" anxiety directly in Week 1. Two-thirds of the class carry this concern. An explicit framing — that the course is designed for this starting point — and a low-stakes early win will matter more than any technical content in Week 1.
  • Command line literacy is the weakest link. 60% rated themselves 1 or 2. Students may have VS Code open without knowing how to navigate their file system or run a script. A focused, hands-on terminal session early in the quarter will pay dividends.
  • GitHub anxiety is real. 43% flagged "working in public on GitHub" as a concern — nearly as many as flagged pace. Normalize the "commit small, commit often" mindset and emphasize that rough commits are part of the craft.
  • The AI tool question needs a clear answer early. Two students raised cost concerns about paid AI subscriptions. Whether the course requires a paid tier or not, the policy should be explicit by the first session.
  • Address the vibecoding-vs-fundamentals question head-on. At least one student is worried about cutting corners they don't know they're cutting. Your framing of why AI-assisted learning still builds real skills — and how students can go deeper if they want — deserves airtime in the course introduction.
  • The cohort is already AI-native. 73% use Claude or a similar tool already. You can move faster on AI tooling basics than a prior cohort might have allowed, and can direct energy toward prompt quality, output evaluation, and workflow integration.
  • Stretch content exists. A subset of students want to go deeper into agents, MCPs, and advanced Claude/Cursor features. Consider optional challenge extensions rather than holding the full class to that pace.
  • UX Research students and data-oriented PMs want a data track. About a third of the class is specifically focused on qualitative/quantitative data analysis for stakeholders. Make sure the data-side exercises feel HCD-relevant, not like generic data science homework.
